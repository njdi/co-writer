Everyone is uncertain
127K
AI is transforming the world as we know it, and like with any big change, people are afraid. Not just the people you’d expect. Everyone. All the way down the stack.
We tend to assume fear lives at the bottom, hitting Junior employees, new grads, and people without leverage. That's not what's happening right now. Uncertainty runs through every single layer and level at the same time.
As someone who works in AI, I have some thoughts about what’s going on at every layer. Hopefully, they provide some validation, some clarity, and of course, some hope:
𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗙𝗼𝘂𝗻𝗱𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗠𝗼𝗱𝗲𝗹𝘀.
The model providers are all competing against each other in a race that resets every few weeks. Leaderboards shift. Benchmarks get dethroned. A new release drops and suddenly someone else is given the crown. The players at the very top, the ones everyone assumes are winning, are still very much in motion, adjusting in real time as the ground moves under them.
𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗔𝗽𝗽 𝗟𝗮𝘆𝗲𝗿.
The companies building on top of the core foundation models feel the pressure too, just in a different way. Every model update or new capability announcement can blur the lines of what’s differentiated. The game becomes building faster, yes, but also building smarter. Creating real lock-in, real network effects, and real value that goes beyond something easily replicated by a prompt.
𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗜𝗻𝗰𝘂𝗺𝗯𝗲𝗻𝘁𝘀.
The legacy players all know what’s coming, but knowing and acting are very different things. They are bolting AI onto existing products and calling it transformation. Agent here, assistant there. They know it’s probably not enough, but they are attempting to buy themselves time.
𝗞𝗻𝗼𝘄𝗹𝗲𝗱𝗴𝗲 𝗪𝗼𝗿𝗸𝗲𝗿𝘀.
Inside every company, the people doing the actual work are watching agents get better by the month. AI mandates from leadership are creating urgency, though not always the most inspiring kind.
So, people adapt. They learn the tools, use them, and experiment with them, both to stay relevant and to stay ahead. There’s a shared understanding that being the person who knows how to work with the tools matters. It makes you useful in a new way.
𝗡𝗲𝘄 𝗚𝗿𝗮𝗱𝘀.
This is the group that feels the current shift most directly. They did everything right. Got the degree, built the resume, even lined up all the right internships. Now, they’re entering a job market that’s changing shape in real time. The traditional on-ramp into a career isn’t gone, but it’s narrower and less predictable. The question floating around hiring conversations is: what does “entry-level” even look like now? This generation isn’t just stepping into the workforce, they’re redefining how the first step works entirely.
Now, it’s very important to look back in time at all the ways people have adapted to unprecedented tech advancement. If you look at psychology, there is a useful distinction between specific fear and diffuse anxiety. Specific fear is narrow and direct. You see a threat, respond, and act. Diffuse anxiety is the opposite. You can’t pinpoint the exact source, so you can’t exactly fight back.
That’s what AI feels like for many people right now. A change in the atmosphere. In the past, technological shifts were more concrete. The web disrupted print. Mobile disrupted the web. You could rationalize things that felt like the future versus soon-to-be relics of the past. Processing the influx of AI is different. It feels like everyone is simultaneously the disruptor and the disrupted.
So, how can we all navigate this time of uncertainty without falling into a state of diffuse anxiety and panic?
𝟭. 𝗙𝗶𝗻𝗱 𝘄𝗼𝗿𝗸 𝘁𝗵𝗮𝘁 𝗴𝗶𝘃𝗲𝘀 𝘆𝗼𝘂 𝗽𝘂𝗿𝗽𝗼𝘀𝗲. Build conviction. Go further than seems rational. When everything is moving fast and nothing feels stable, optimizing for safety starts to look like standing still. The people who hold up over time are the ones pulled forward by something deeper than incentives. They care more, so they stay longer, push harder, and see things through when it would be easier to pivot away.
What problems do you return to without being told? What kind of work do you notice yourself overthinking, refining, pushing past the point of “good enough”? What do you keep circling back to, even when it’s inconvenient to do so?
Purpose doesn’t make things easier, it just makes them worth continuing. In an environment where the rules keep rewriting themselves, that kind of internal fuel and fire matters more than any external playbook.
𝟮. 𝗕𝗲 𝘄𝗶𝗹𝗹𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝘁𝗼 𝘂𝗻𝗹𝗲𝗮𝗿𝗻 𝗮𝘀 𝗳𝗮𝘀𝘁 𝗮𝘀 𝘆𝗼𝘂 𝗹𝗲𝗮𝗿𝗻.
Your playbook is probably already outdated. The tactics that worked two years ago, even two months ago, don’t always transfer cleanly. The half-life of expertise is shrinking, and what used to be an advantage can harden into habit.
The people who stay sharp are the ones who can let go the fastest. The ones who can admit something no longer works and move on without overidentifying with it. The practice of remaining open-minded and curious has never been so necessary.
Cling to what you know too strongly and it could become an anchor that sinks you.
𝟯. 𝗙𝗶𝗻𝗱 𝘆𝗼𝘂𝗿 𝘁𝗿𝗶𝗯𝗲.
People feel more isolated than they let on. Between remote work, algorithmic feeds, echo-chambers, and the general anxiety that never fully turns off, people everywhere are being nudged inward, into smaller and smaller loops.
It is critical that you push against that.
Seek out people who are building, thinking, questioning in the same direction. Find the ones who remain intrigued by the basic and seemingly mundane. Seek out people who challenge you, who widen your frame, who make the whole thing feel less abstract. Careers are rarely built alone, especially now. The relationships you form become the people who open doors, keep you steady, spread the word about what you’re doing, and tell you the truth when you need it.
The unknown is always feared. When that fear is defined, it becomes a compass, giving you direction. Right now, no one has a map. The coordinates are still being plotted.
This isn’t a moment to wait and see. It’s not the time to look away or give up. It’s a moment to participate. To stay close to the work, to the people, and to the questions that matter to you.
In a landscape that keeps shifting, the only real advantage is being willing to move and adapt with it.
