here's 11 proven methods you can use to start monetising the biggest gold rush (AI) today. it's time to lock in.
"tools are cheap, outcomes are expensive"
In 2026, the better AI businesses will not sell dashboards, prompts or “AI access”. They will sell booked jobs, resolved tickets, recovered revenue, finished reports and work that actually gets done.
The market is moving from software to machine labour.
The money is going straight into the pockets of the people that are able to sell the outcomes.
Here are the 11 models worth paying attention to.

1. AI Audits: The easiest wedge into real consulting money

AI audits are still one of the cleanest entry points.
Not because the audit itself is magic.
Because most businesses have no idea where AI should go inside their operations.
A good AI audit does one thing well:
It finds the leaks.
Missed calls. Slow admin. Repeated customer questions. Manual quoting. Messy follow-ups. Dead CRM records. Staff copying information between tools like it is still 2012.
That is where the money is hiding.
The smart version uses a voice agent to interview the owner or operations manager for 20 to 30 minutes. It asks about daily workflows, bottlenecks, customer complaints, staff workload, missed revenue and all the tiny admin fires nobody wants to own.
Then the transcript becomes a practical map:
  • What is broken
  • What can be automated
  • What should stay human
  • What is cheap to fix
  • What has the biggest financial upside
A £800 to £1,500 audit can work as a paid diagnosis. But the audit is not the main business. The real money comes after it: implementation, retainers, managed automation and ongoing optimisation.
This is a strong beginner model if you can sell, listen and translate messy operations into simple recommendations.
You do not need to be the best engineer in the room.
You need to be the person who can say:
“Here is where you are losing £8,000 a month. Here is the first fix.”
That is valuable.

2. Local AI SaaS: Boring businesses, beautiful retention

Plumbers do not care about agentic workflows.
They care about booked jobs.
Dentists do not care about your AI stack.
They care about missed calls, reviews, reminders and filled calendars.
That is why local AI SaaS still works.
You take white-label infrastructure, package it around a painful local-business problem, then charge monthly.
The classic example is missed-call text back.
HighLevel has promoted the idea that 62% of calls to small businesses go unanswered, and other small-business call analyses point to the same ugly truth: missed calls turn into lost revenue fast.
For a roofer, dentist, med spa, estate agent or HVAC company, one missed call is not “a small admin issue”.
It might be a £600 job.
It might be a £4,000 treatment plan.
It might be a customer who goes straight to the next Google result.
The offer is simple:
  • Missed-call text back
  • AI receptionist
  • Review requests
  • Google Business Profile support
  • Appointment reminders
  • Basic CRM follow-up
  • Monthly reporting
This model wins because it sells recovered revenue, not software.
Typical pricing can sit around $297 to $497 per month for basic packages, depending on the market, niche and depth of service.
But there is a trap.
Being lazy and calling it a niche.
If you sell the same generic “AI automation package” to every business, you become a commodity. If you specialise in one vertical and learn its language, you become harder to replace.
Do not be “AI for local business”.
Be:
  • Missed-job recovery for emergency plumbers
  • Patient reactivation for cosmetic dentists
  • After-hours lead capture for estate agents
  • Review generation for med spas
  • Quote follow-up for roofers
Specificity is the difference between a forgettable AI agency and a business people actually remember.
That is where the retention comes from.

3. AI Creative Agencies: The solo operator now has agency leverage

This is where things get strange.
A single operator with the right AI video workflow can now produce work that would have needed a production crew, studio, editor, animator and a painful invoice two years ago.
Higgsfield Ads 2.0, for example, focuses on generating polished product commercials from product images, with product placement and ad-style presets built into the workflow.
That matters because advertising production has always had a cost problem.
Location costs. Crew costs. Reshoots. Product shots. Lighting. Editing. Motion graphics. Revisions that make everyone quietly hate the project by week three.
AI does not remove taste.
It removes drag.
A good AI creative operator can now offer:
  • Spec ads for brands
  • Short-form product videos
  • UGC-style ad variants
  • CGI product scenes
  • Social ad packs
  • Landing-page hero videos
  • Founder-led launch assets
The client acquisition play is obvious:
Make spec ads.
Pick recognisable brands. Create work they would never expect from a solo creator. Post it on X and LinkedIn. Make the result undeniable.
Do not beg for attention.
Build something worth looking at.
But here is where most people get this wrong:
The market will not reward “AI video”. It will reward AI video that sells the product.
That is a different skill.
Most creators will make shiny nonsense. The winners will understand offers, buyer psychology, scroll behaviour, hooks, product angles and testing.

4. Niche Digital Products: The unlimited slot machine, with one catch

Digital products are still a monster market.
But the old version is tired.
“Make a course about productivity” is dead unless you already have trust, distribution or a slightly terrifying cult following.
The better play is narrow utility.
Not:
“How to make money online.” (kek at the title of this article, hey, it's free)
More like:
  • Fortnite map monetisation templates
  • Crisis comms trackers for school administrators
  • AI prompt packs for conveyancing assistants
  • Notion dashboards for boutique wedding planners
  • Boilerplates for Shopify app developers
  • Client onboarding kits for fractional CFOs
That is the move.
Oddly specific beats broadly helpful.
Whop’s case study on Iman Gadzhi’s Monetise product shows the modern version of this model: not a lonely PDF, but a stacked ecosystem with training, tools, community and AI-assisted product creation.
A digital product is no longer just information.
It is a shortcut packaged as a system.
AI helps with research, structure, sales pages, scripts, lead magnets, email sequences and fulfilment.
But do not confuse easy production with easy demand.
That is the bit beginners get wrong.
You can create the thing faster now. That does not mean anyone wants it.
The hard bit is still taste.
You need to spot a real pain. You need to know what buyers already tried. You need to package the solution in a way that feels specific enough to be made for them.
The best digital products in 2026 will feel weirdly precise.
Like someone overheard the buyer complaining at 11:47pm and built the fix overnight.
That is the standard.

5. Growth Engineering: Marketing becomes a system, not a mood

Traditional marketing teams still waste too much time doing middle-work.
Building variants. Pulling reports. Scraping research. Formatting ads. Uploading campaigns. Turning off losers. Making another spreadsheet that will be outdated by Thursday.
Growth engineering attacks that layer.
Instead of manually creating five ad variants, you build a pipeline that creates 500.
Not random junk.
Variants based on real objections, pain points, competitor complaints and customer language.
This is where coding agents and campaign APIs become dangerous in the right hands.
Operators are already using Claude Code-style workflows, Meta Ads automation and bulk creative generation to compress campaign execution. Recent guides describe AI-assisted systems for generating large numbers of ad variations and managing campaign operations through APIs.
The workflow looks like this:
  • Scrape Reddit, reviews, forums and sales calls
  • Extract pains, objections and buying triggers
  • Turn those into hooks and angles
  • Generate ad variants
  • Launch controlled tests
  • Kill weak performers
  • Scale winners
  • Feed learning back into the system
The edge is not “AI wrote my ad”.
The edge is a machine that learns faster than your competitors can meet.
This model suits technical marketers.
Not pure coders.
Not pure copywriters.
The winners are hybrids. People who understand APIs, data, direct response, paid social and buyer psychology.
Rare breed.
Good.
Rare breeds get paid.

6. RAG-Lite Knowledge Systems: The unsexy moat

Most small businesses do not need a complex enterprise RAG system.
They need their own company brain.
A place where policies, sales calls, SOPs, client notes, proposals, research, meeting notes and internal decisions can be searched, queried and reused.
Classic Retrieval-Augmented Generation connects language models to external knowledge sources so outputs can draw on relevant data rather than only model training. AWS describes RAG as a way to reference authoritative knowledge bases before generating responses.
Useful idea.
Often overbuilt.
Full RAG can be too much for smaller teams.
Vector databases. Embeddings. Pipelines. Permissions. Maintenance. Costs. Fragility.
Most teams do not need a cathedral.
They need clean plumbing.
RAG-lite is the practical cousin.
Think structured markdown, Obsidian vaults, clean folders, naming conventions and an instruction file that tells the AI how to navigate.
A simple structure might be:
  • raw: unprocessed notes, PDFs, transcripts and rough captures
  • wiki: cleaned knowledge pages
  • clients: account-specific information
  • offers: products, services, pricing and positioning
  • outputs: generated answers, drafts and reports
  • logs: decision history, updates and recurring notes

7. AI Influencers and Synthetic Content Channels: Scalable, but not effortless

AI influencers are no longer just novelty accounts.
They are becoming owned media assets.
A synthetic persona can post daily, appear in video, promote products, run affiliate offers, build lore and create audience familiarity without the fatigue of a human creator.
But beginners miss the main point.
The character is not the business.
The character is the costume. Distribution is the machine.
You still need positioning.
You still need hooks.
You still need story.
You still need a reason for people to care.
The same applies to YouTube automation.
AI can now help with scripts, voice-overs, images, animation and editing. But low-effort AI spam is easier to spot and easier to ignore.
The stronger operators use human-in-the-loop workflows:
  • AI drafts the script
  • Human fixes the angle
  • AI generates scenes
  • Human checks pacing
  • AI builds voice and assets
  • Human edits for retention
  • AI repurposes into shorts
  • Human studies comments and analytics
That is where taste lives.
And taste is still expensive.

8. Micro-SaaS With Outcome-Based Pricing: The cleanest business model in the stack

This is the big one.
Seat-based SaaS made sense when humans were the users.
But when an agent does the work, the seat becomes a strange billing unit.
a16z has argued that AI is pushing software towards outcome-based pricing because AI systems can perform work that used to be tied to human seats.
Nevermined has also written about the infrastructure needed for outcome pricing, including metering, settlement and audit capabilities for AI agents.
This is the model I would pay the most attention to.
Not:
“$99 per month for dashboard access.”
More like:
  • £30 per qualified inbound lead
  • £200 per booked roofing appointment
  • 5% of recovered abandoned-cart revenue
  • £15 per resolved support ticket
  • £500 per compliant supplier audit completed
  • £2 per verified enriched contact
Outcome pricing works because it speaks the buyer’s language.
A roofing contractor does not want another login.
They want booked inspections.
A recruiter does not want an “AI sourcing assistant”.
They want qualified candidates.
A law firm does not want document automation in theory.
They want completed first drafts, reviewed by a human, ready by 9am.
Outcome pricing aligns the vendor with the buyer’s wallet.
That is why it is powerful.
But it also creates a harder business.
The danger is attribution.
Who gets credit for the lead? What counts as qualified? What happens if the client’s sales team fumbles the appointment? What happens if the lead books, ghosts, then comes back three weeks later?
This is where contracts, tracking and audit trails matter.
Outcome-based pricing is not just a pricing model.
It is an accountability system.
And that is why most people will avoid it.
Good.
That leaves room for the operators who can handle the mess.

9. Design-as-a-Service: Canva plus AI eats the boring design queue

Most business design is not art.
It is production.
Resize this. Turn this into a carousel. Make a flyer. Update the pitch deck. Create a thumbnail. Reformat the case study. Build 20 ad variants. Keep it on brand. Do it by tomorrow.
This is exactly where AI-assisted design services fit.
Canva’s AI Connector, powered by its MCP Server, allows assistants such as Claude and ChatGPT to connect with Canva so users can create, edit, manage and search content from chat.
That is a big deal because the old AI image problem was editability.
You could generate an image, but changing one word often meant regenerating the whole thing.
Structured design tools solve that.
The operator can now work inside editable brand assets, not flattened images.
A strong Design-as-a-Service offer might include:
  • 30 social posts per month
  • 10 ad creatives
  • 4 email graphics
  • 2 pitch deck updates
  • Brand kit enforcement
  • Thumbnail variants
  • Monthly creative testing report
The value is not “AI made a design”. The value is brand-consistent output at ridiculous speed.
This model is good for operators who understand layout, brand systems and client management.
Bad designers will still produce bad work faster.
Good designers will finally stop drowning in repetitive production.
That is the real win.

10. AI Video Contests and Partner Programmes: Prize money as seed funding

AI video contests are becoming a real economic lane.
Not for everyone.
For elite creators, they can be a way to win cash, earn status, get partner access and attract serious inbound work.
The Higgsfield AI Action Contest reportedly had a $500,000 prize pool, with first place receiving $150,000 foir “
GRANDMA vs WASP
” by Muhannad Nassar and Simon Meyer (click grandma vs wasp to see it).
That is not pocket money lmao.
But the best entries are not simple text-to-video experiments.
They require cinematic judgement:
  • Story structure
  • Shot design
  • Character consistency
  • Motion control
  • Compositing
  • Editing
  • Sound
  • Taste
That then acts as proof.
That proof can become inbound work, partner access, consulting, workshops, production retainers or a specialist studio offer.
The downside?
This is not a stable beginner model.
It is closer to professional sport.
High upside. Brutal competition. Inconsistent income.
Use contests as leverage, not as your whole business.
Win attention.
Then turn that attention into something that compounds.

11. Mobile and Edge AI Agents: Powerful, but handle with care

This section needs to stay sober.
The bad version of this market is obvious:
“Make automation look human.” “Bypass platform restrictions.” “Run things under the radar.”
That is not a durable business thesis.
That is a ban waiting to happen.
The legitimate opportunity is better:
Run agents closer to the user, the device and the local workflow.
Hermes Agent documentation, for example, describes running an agent on Android through Termux, giving users a local CLI environment on the phone.
That opens up practical use cases:
  • Local file workflows
  • Personal automation
  • Private device-side assistants
  • Field-team support
  • SMS-based internal reminders
  • On-device research capture
  • Lightweight mobile operations
  • Remote worker task support
This is especially interesting for businesses where the phone is still the real operating system.
Trades. Field sales. Logistics. Property. Events. Clinics. Local services.
The commercial angle should be governance, not evasion.
The winning offer is not:
“We can trick platforms.”
The winning offer is:
“We can automate mobile workflows without losing control, privacy or compliance.”
That is a better business.
It survives scrutiny.
It keeps clients safe.
And it lets you charge premium engineering fees without sounding like you are one bad sales deck away from a lawsuit.

SO... HOW SHOULD I MAKE MONEY WITH AI?

well, I gave you 11 proven ideas.. but the honest answer.
It depends on your skillset.
Annoying answer, I know.
Still true.
If you can sell but cannot build deeply
Start with:
  • AI audits
  • Local AI SaaS
  • Design-as-a-Service
  • Simple automation retainers
These are easier to enter because the tech stack already exists.
Your job is packaging, trust, diagnosis and delivery.
The risk is commoditisation.
If every agency in your city can offer the same missed-call bot, your margin gets squeezed.
So specialise.
Pick a niche. Learn its economics. Speak its language.

If you can build and think commercially

Look at:
  • Growth engineering
  • RAG-lite systems
  • Outcome-based Micro-SaaS
  • Edge automation
These models are more defensible because they require deeper implementation skill.
They are harder to copy from a YouTube tutorial.
They also create more operational lock-in.
That matters.
The deeper your system sits inside the client’s workflow, the harder it is to replace.

If you are a creator with taste...

Pay attention to:
  • AI creative agencies
  • Synthetic content channels
  • AI video contests
  • Niche digital products
But do not become a tool demo account.
That is death.
Nobody needs another person posting “insane AI video workflow” with the same glossy astronaut clip and no commercial point.
Build an audience around taste, results, story and proof.
That is the difference.

MY FAVOURITES...

1. Outcome-Based Micro-SaaS

Because it aligns with how buyers actually think.
They do not want software.
They want leads, booked calls, resolved tickets, recovered revenue, completed reports and fewer fires.
If your AI system creates a measurable business result, you can charge against that result.
That is clean.
Not easy.
Clean.

2. RAG-Lite Knowledge Systems

Because every company is drowning in internal knowledge.
Their best ideas are scattered across Slack, Notion, Google Drive, email threads, call transcripts and the heads of three overworked employees.
Help them turn that mess into a usable company brain and you become more than a vendor.
You become infrastructure.
McKinsey has warned that as AI systems become more autonomous and embedded in critical workflows, governance and monitoring become increasingly important.
That is exactly why knowledge architecture matters.
The more autonomy companies adopt, the more they need clean context, strong rules and reliable memory.
The agent is only as good as the context it can trust.
Bad context creates expensive stupidity.
Good context creates leverage.

AI is not the moat.
Access is not the moat.

  3. Charge close to the outcome. The closer your pricing sits to revenue, savings or risk reduction, the stronger the business.
